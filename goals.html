How to Set Goals That Stick

Clarity is worthless until it becomes action. Here's how to set goals across the four areas of your life and reverse-engineer any one-year goal all the way down to what you'll do this week.

By Karan Jugran, Living Foundations · Part of the Clarity series

Your mission comes from two sources: natural interests you've always had, and voids — painful gaps from your past that drive you to fill them for others. Most people's life purpose lives in one of these two places. Once you know your direction, goals are how you actually move.

The four life areas

Set goals in all four areas for a complete picture of your life. Neglect one and it tends to quietly undermine the rest.

Area 1

Health

Physical fitness, plus mental wellbeing and stress.

Area 2

Relationships

Close personal bonds and your wider social and professional circles.

Area 3

Career

Passion projects and craft, plus professional service and skills.

Area 4

Finance

Active income and growth, plus passive income and investments.

The goal breakdown system

Take your one-year goal and reverse-engineer it all the way down to today. Each level gets smaller and more concrete, until the final step is something you could literally start now.

  1. 1 year — the big goal.
  2. 9 months — the major milestone.
  3. 6 months — the halfway checkpoint.
  4. 3 months — a concrete quarterly target.
  5. 1 month — the monthly habit or milestone.
  6. This week — one action you'll actually do.

Every Sunday, review your week. Too easy? Push harder next week. Too hard? Dial it back. You're training your ability to estimate your own capacity — and that self-knowledge is itself a superpower.

Where your drive actually comes from

Before you write down a single goal, it helps to know why you want it. Almost everyone's direction traces back to one of two sources.

The first is natural interest — something you leaned toward before anyone told you to. A kid who's handed a pen and starts doodling instead of stopping. A kid who plays sport from morning to night and never seems to tire of it. Someone who's always taken gadgets apart just to understand what's inside. Nobody assigned any of this. It's just there.

The second, and often the stronger of the two, is a void — a gap or a pain point from your own life that you're now driven to close. A kid who was physically weak and got sick after any light exercise can grow into someone who studies nutrition and training obsessively, not because fitness was a childhood passion, but because the lack of it hurt. The classic example is an entrepreneur who spends decades funding meals for people in poverty — not because feeding people was ever their hobby, but because someone once fed their own family when they had nothing, and that moment never left them. The mission gets built around the wound, not the hobby.

Neither source is better than the other, and most people are running on some mix of both. Look back at what you wrote for your one-year goals above — notice which of your goals come from something you've always loved, and which come from something you've always lacked. Both are valid reasons to chase something.

Should you do this alone?

If you have the means, hiring a coach for the area you're working on is usually a smarter use of your time than teaching yourself from scratch. Think of it the way you'd think about hiring help at home: you can learn to cook yourself, but unless cooking is a genuine passion, most people would rather pay someone who already knows how, and spend the saved time elsewhere. The same logic applies to a trainer, a financial advisor, or anyone further along the path you're trying to walk.

A good coach doesn't just hand you a plan — you get their eye for your specific mistakes, an outside source of motivation on the days yours runs out, and the accountability of someone checking in on you. None of that replaces the daily work described above; it just removes the months you'd otherwise spend figuring things out through trial and error on your own.

If you're doing this for the first time, don't start bigger than a one-year goal — six months is fine if a full year feels like too much to picture. Once you've run the whole process once, your sense of pacing improves, and stretching to an eighteen-month or two-year goal gets much easier to break down accurately.

Key takeaways

  • Know your source. Your goals come from natural interest, a void you're trying to fill, or some mix of both — name which one is driving each goal you wrote down.
  • Cover all four areas. Health, relationships, career, and finance each need a one-year goal, or the neglected one will quietly drag on the rest.
  • Start from a realistic baseline, not the end state. Design month one around where you actually are today, not the version of you that already hit the goal.
  • Reverse-engineer in stages. One year to nine months to six to three to one month to one week to one day — each step should feel only slightly harder than the last.
  • Review every single week. Adjust difficulty up or down based on what actually happened, not what you hoped would happen.
  • Get outside help where it counts. A coach doesn't do the work for you — it just removes the time you'd waste learning alone.

Purpose isn't always born from loving a thing — sometimes it's born from its absence.

Frequently asked questions

Which areas of life should I set goals in?

Set goals across four life areas for a complete picture: health (physical and mental), relationships (close bonds and wider circles), career (craft and professional skills), and finance (active income and passive investments).

How do you break a big goal into steps?

Start with your one-year goal, then reverse-engineer it: 9-month milestone, 6-month checkpoint, 3-month target, 1-month habit, and finally one concrete action for this week. Each level gets smaller and more concrete.

How often should I review my goals?

Review every week. If the week was too easy, push harder next week; if too hard, dial it back. Over time you train your ability to estimate your own capacity — a skill worth as much as the goals themselves.

Is it worth hiring a coach instead of doing this alone?

If you have the means, yes for whichever area you're weakest in. A coach gives you an outside eye on your mistakes, accountability, and motivation, and it removes the months you'd otherwise spend learning through trial and error alone. It doesn't replace the daily work — it just speeds up how fast you improve at it.
