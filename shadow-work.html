Shadow Work: The Part of You You've Been Hiding From

In psychology, the "shadow" is the side of your personality your conscious mind doesn't recognise or accept. Not because you're bad — but because life taught you to push it away.

By Karan Jugran, Living Foundations · Part of the Clarity series

Ignored, the shadow quietly runs the show from behind the scenes — the anger you can't explain, the pattern you keep repeating, the reaction that surprises even you. Faced honestly, those same parts become your greatest source of wholeness.

Why do shadow work?

Your blind spots run your life whether you look at them or not. The gambler who can't see his addiction. The resentment that leaks into every relationship. Shadow work is shining a light on what's been running in the dark — because you can't improve what you can't see.

This is sensitive territory. There are no wrong answers — only honest ones and comfortable lies. The more you squirm writing something down, the more important it probably is.

Three ways to find your shadow

  1. Meditation. Sit quietly and observe your patterns and reactions without judgment. Notice what feelings arise around certain people, memories, or situations.
  2. Ask people who know you deeply. Go to 3–5 close friends or family and ask: "What 3–5 things about me do you feel hold me back?" Where their answers sting or surprise you — that's your shadow.
  3. Shadow journaling. Answer specific questions fully and honestly. These questions act as a torch, shining light on the parts of your mind you've kept in the dark.

Honest prompts to begin

A few of the questions from the Clarity shadow-work set. Sit with the ones that make you uncomfortable:

  • What do you hate most in other people? (What you despise in others is often what you've suppressed in yourself.)
  • What consistently triggers your anger or irritation — even in small moments?
  • What are you most ashamed of — things you hope no one ever asks you about?
  • Pick a trait you consider negative. What's something good about it?

The goal of shadow work isn't to fix yourself. It's to see yourself fully — the whole person, not just the parts you approve of. A tree's highest branches require the deepest roots.

What your reactions to others are telling you

Your shadow rarely announces itself directly — it shows up sideways, in reactions that feel bigger than the moment deserves. If someone who talks too much irritates you far more than it should, it may be because you were once shamed for talking too much and quietly shut that part of yourself down. The trait didn't disappear. It just moved somewhere you stopped looking.

A few places worth checking. Notice the topics you avoid or change the subject on — death and money are common ones — because discomfort around a topic usually means something unresolved sits underneath it. Ask yourself what you'd take even at someone else's expense; the answer exposes self-interest you'd rather believe isn't there. And pay attention to the trait in other people that provokes the strongest reaction in you — someone who "doesn't listen to anyone" and just does their own thing, for example. That reaction can mask a hidden envy of the very freedom you denied yourself.

One distinction matters here: not everything uncomfortable is shadow. Knowing you have a sweet tooth and keeping it in check is just self-awareness. It only becomes shadow material when you deny it exists at all — when you can't see it, rather than simply choosing to manage it.

Every trait cuts both ways

Shadow work isn't a search-and-destroy mission. Almost every trait you dislike — in yourself or in someone else — carries both a cost and a benefit, and seeing both sides is the point. Not listening to others can look inconsiderate, but it also means faster decisions and less second-guessing. Listening carefully builds trust and connection, but it can slow you down and lead you to override your own instincts just to keep the group happy. Neither side is the "true" one. Both are real, and pretending only one exists is exactly how a trait ends up in the shadow in the first place.

Every person carries both the admirable parts they're happy to show and the denied parts they'd rather not look at. Until you can see the second half as clearly as the first, it doesn't go away — it just keeps running your life from behind the scenes, unseen and unmanaged. The aim isn't to erase what you find. It's to see it clearly enough to choose your response instead of being run by it.

Every person carries both the "god" and the "devil" within them. Everyone wants to display the god. But until you can also see the devil, it doesn't disappear — it just keeps running your life from behind the scenes.

Key takeaways

  • The shadow is ordinary, not pathological. It's the denied or suppressed material every person carries, not some exotic affliction.
  • Awareness comes first. You can't fix or integrate what you refuse to see — the first task is simply noticing it.
  • Start with meditation or honest outside eyes. Sit with your own reactions, or ask 3–5 close people what they honestly see in you.
  • Your reactions are clues. What you hate in others, what topics you avoid, and what triggers a disproportionate response often point straight at a blind spot.
  • Overlap is signal. When multiple people independently name the same trait, take it seriously — that's not one opinion, it's a pattern.
  • Every trait has two sides. The goal isn't to erase your shadow but to see its cost and benefit clearly enough to choose your response instead of being run by it.

Frequently asked questions

What is shadow work?

Shadow work is examining the parts of your personality your conscious mind doesn't recognise or accept — the hidden side shaped by what life taught you to push away. Faced honestly, these parts become a source of wholeness instead of running your life from the background.

Why should I do shadow work?

Your blind spots run your life whether you look at them or not. Shadow work shines a light on what's been operating in the dark — and you can't improve what you can't see.

How do I start shadow work?

Three ways: quiet meditation to observe your reactions, asking people who know you well what holds you back, and shadow journaling — answering specific honest questions. The more honest you are, the more you'll find.

Is the goal to fix myself?

No. The goal is not to eliminate parts of yourself but to see yourself fully — the whole person, not just the parts you approve of. Integration, not elimination.
