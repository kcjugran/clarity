Part 1

Your Ikigai

Ikigai is a Japanese concept meaning "reason for being." A popular framework breaks your life purpose into four overlapping circles — and where they all meet is your Ikigai.

Circle 1

What you love

Activities where time disappears. Pure joy — nothing to do with how skilled you are.

Circle 2

What you're good at

Natural strengths or built skills. People notice. You feel genuinely confident here.

Circle 3

What the world needs

Problems that bother you deeply. Gaps you see that others seem to ignore.

Circle 4

What you can be paid for

Skills with real market value — now or with time invested.

Where circles overlap
Love + Good at = Passion
Good at + Paid for = Profession
Paid for + World needs = Vocation
Love + World needs = Mission

Your goal is to find the one thing sitting at the centre of all four. These questions are a one-time deep exercise — they take 45 minutes to 2 hours. Do them with full focus.

What you love
1
What are 3–5 activities — from childhood or now — where time completely disappeared for you?
Aim for 5. More answers = deeper insight.
Think of moments where you looked up and were shocked hours had passed.
2
What are 3–5 hobbies you've had from childhood to now — things others would always find you doing?
3
What 3–5 experiences have you had that you wish everyone would try — things the world doesn't talk about enough?
4
Name 3–5 people you deeply admire. Next to each name, write one sentence on why.
What you admire in others often reflects what you secretly want to express yourself.
What you're good at
5
What 3–5 things are you consistently complimented on?
6
What 3–5 tasks — if someone challenged you right now — would you say "absolutely, no problem"?
7
What do people consistently ask you for help with? What problem do they bring to you?
8
If a company had all your data and assigned you 3–5 job profiles you'd naturally succeed in — what would they be?
9
If you had unlimited money, the world's best teachers, and 3–5 years — what field could you reach an exceptional level in?
This is a hard question. Take time with it.
What the world needs
10
Who are 3–5 people across all of history who you feel have done the most good for humanity — and why?
11
What are the 3–5 biggest problems you see in the world today? Why does each bother you more than others?
12
If you could introduce 3–5 laws for your country or the world, what would they be — and why?
13
What 3–5 mistakes do people make most often in life — things you wish they'd stop doing?
What you can be paid for
14
What 3–5 fields do you see where people seem genuinely happy AND earning well?
15
What 3–5 skills do you have (or nearly have) where you feel real money is possible with more time?
16
What 3–5 fields, if you spent 3–5 years learning them, feel like they'd pay very well with little financial risk?
17
What 3–5 professions feel like there's no ceiling on earning — the harder you work, the more unlimited the upside?

Part 2

Surfacing your real values

Life purpose isn't assigned to you from the outside. It's built from your values — the things you actually spend time, money, and energy on without anyone pushing you. Not what you say matters, but what you do.

What a value really is

A value is something you do automatically — no motivation needed. Watch your actual behaviour, not your stated beliefs. If you say you value money but never save — money isn't truly high on your value scale yet.

The 5 clarity questions — your daily practice

These 5 questions take 25 minutes total (5 minutes each). Do them every day without looking at yesterday's answers. After 2 weeks, the repeating patterns are your true values.

1
If you had ₹1 crore to spend in a year (savings aside), how would you divide it?
2
If a genie gave you unlimited wishes — what would you wish for? No filter.
3
What are your risky rewards — things you'd do if fear wasn't in the way?
4
What are your biggest current pains — the things eating at you most?
5
Take top 3 from each question (12 points). With only 6 months — what would you do for each?

The final step of question 5: ask yourself — "What is one practical thing I can do TODAY?" Write it down. Then go do it.

Do this every day for at least 2 weeks. Set a 5-minute timer per question. Don't read yesterday's answers — start fresh.

1
If you had ₹1 crore to spend in one year (savings already set aside) — how would you divide it?
List every category and an estimated amount. Keep going until the crore is fully allocated.
2
If a genie appeared and granted you unlimited wishes — what would you wish for?
No filter, no judgment. Silly or serious. Write everything that comes.
3
What are your risky rewards — things you deeply want but haven't done because fear is in the way?
If all risk were removed, what would you do?
4
What are your biggest pains right now — the things in your life eating at you most?
Relationships, health, money, work — be honest. Then rank them.
5
Take your top 3 from each question above (12 points). With only 6 months to improve each — what would you actually do?
End with: one thing you can do TODAY.
The daily action is the most important line you write. Keep it small and concrete — one call, one search, one email.

Bonus

Shadow work

The part of you you've been hiding from

Every person has a shadow — the suppressed, hidden parts of yourself you don't acknowledge. Not because you're bad, but because life taught you to push them away. Ignored, they control you silently. Faced, they become your greatest source of wholeness.

Why do shadow work?

Your blind spots run your life whether you look at them or not. The gambler who can't see his addiction. The anger you can't explain. Shadow work is shining a light on what's been running in the dark. You can't improve what you can't see.

Three ways to find your shadow

1

Meditation

Sit quietly and observe your patterns and reactions without judgment. Notice what feelings arise around certain people, memories, or situations.

2

Ask people who know you deeply

Go to 3–5 close friends or family. Ask: "What 3–5 things about me do you feel hold me back?" Where their answers sting or surprise you — that's your shadow.

3

Shadow journaling

Answer specific questions fully and honestly. These questions act as a torch — shining light on the parts of your mind you've kept in the dark. The more honest you are, the more you'll find.

These questions require real honesty. There are no wrong answers — only honest ones and comfortable lies. The more you squirm writing something, the more important it is.

1
What do you hate most in other people?
What you despise in others is often what you've suppressed in yourself.
Ask yourself: was there ever a time I did this same thing, or secretly wanted to?
2
What consistently triggers your anger or irritation — even in small moments?
Strong reactions usually have a story. When did this wound first happen?
3
What topics make you anxious or want to change the subject?
4
What are you most ashamed of — things you hope no one ever asks you about?
5
What would you do at someone else's expense to get something you deeply want?
Be honest — this reveals hidden desires and moral blind spots.
6
What negative traits in others secretly impress or attract you?
What you admire in the "wrong" people is often a quality you've forbidden yourself to have.
7
What are the hidden benefits of your most negative traits or habits?
Your shadow isn't evil. It was trying to protect you. What was it protecting you from?

The goal of shadow work isn't to fix yourself. It's to see yourself fully — the whole person, not just the parts you approve of. A tree's highest branches require the deepest roots.

Bonus

How to journal

Journaling is downloading your thoughts onto paper — turning the noise in your head into written form so you can actually see it, revisit it, and work with it. Done right, it's the most powerful tool you have for clarity.

Journaling vs diary writing

These are not the same thing, and the difference matters.

Diary

Stream of consciousness. "Today I woke up at 8, had a fight with X, didn't get my promotion." No direction. Pure download.

Journaling

Question-driven. Intentional. You ask your mind something specific and let it answer. It has direction and purpose.

Think of it as your best friend

Journaling is like having a best friend who always has time for you, never judges, asks great questions, and has unlimited patience. The difference from a real friend? Your journal:

✓
Never has a bad day or bad mood
✓
Never gives you biased advice based on their own life
✓
Always focuses entirely on you and your question
✓
Remembers everything you've told it — it's all written down

The real superpower: better questions

The quality of your journal is determined entirely by the quality of questions you ask. Ask small questions, get small answers. Ask big, honest questions and your mind opens up.

Example of a powerful question

"If I only had one year left to live — how would I spend it? What would I stop doing? What would I finally start?"

This single question dissolves petty worries and instantly clarifies what actually matters to you.

Morning vs evening journaling

Both are valuable — but they serve different purposes.

Morning
Sets the direction for your day
Deep perspective questions work best
Identify your one most important action
Tunes your mindset before the noise hits
Evening
Reflects on what happened
What did I learn? What am I proud of?
Closes the day with intention
Sets up tomorrow's first action

Practical notes

Format
Phone, laptop, or pen and paper — all work. Handwriting engages the brain slightly more deeply, but the best format is the one you'll actually use.
Time
5 minutes on a quiet day. 30 minutes on an eventful one. Let the question decide.
Consistency
A journal done daily at an average level beats a brilliant journal done occasionally. The habit is the point.
Questions
Mix daily reflection questions (what happened today) with evergreen perspective questions (the big life ones). Both matter.

The most successful, clear-minded people journal consistently — not because they have more interesting lives, but because they process their lives more intentionally. Journaling is how a wild, scattered mind learns to run in a direction.

Use these templates as a starting point. Adapt them — remove what doesn't fit, add questions that matter to you. Your journal should feel like yours.

Morning template

Do this before you check your phone. Sets the tone for the whole day. Takes 10–15 minutes.

M1
What is the one most important thing I must do today?
If you do nothing else — what's the one action that moves your life forward most?
Everything else in your day is secondary to this. Protect time for it first.
M2
If I only had one year left to live — how would I spend today differently?
A perspective question to dissolve small worries and bring what truly matters into focus.
You don't need to act on everything — just let the question shift your lens for the day.
M3
If my wisest self could give me two pieces of advice for today — what would they be?
Imagine a version of you who has already lived through today perfectly. What do they know?
M4
What am I looking forward to today — even something small?
Starting the day by finding something to anticipate trains your mind toward positivity without faking it.
Evening template

Do this before bed. Closes the day with reflection and sets up tomorrow. Takes 10–20 minutes.

E1
What are three things I learned today?
Big or small — anything that was new information, a new skill, or a new perspective.
E2
What are three things I could have done better today?
Not self-criticism — honest observation. Where did you leave room on the table?
The goal isn't guilt — it's calibration. You're becoming a better observer of yourself.
E3
What am I proud of today?
No standard required. It can be tiny. It's yours.
Pride doesn't need comparison to others. Something as small as "I went to bed on time" absolutely counts.
E4
What am I genuinely excited about right now?
E5
What was one interesting or unexpected observation from today?
Something you noticed, heard, read, or felt that felt worth remembering.
This is your curiosity muscle. Some days it'll be empty. Some days it'll surprise you.
E6
What is my one power action for tomorrow?
The single thing that — if done — would make tomorrow genuinely well spent.
This is the bridge between today and tomorrow. Don't skip it.
Perspective questions — use any time

These are deeper, evergreen questions. Pick one and sit with it for 10–20 minutes. They're designed to shift how you see things, not just describe what happened.

"If a wise, kind version of me looked at my life right now — what would they lovingly tell me to stop doing? What would they encourage me to start?"

Good for: getting unstuck, cutting through self-deception

"What am I tolerating in my life right now that I know I shouldn't be? What am I waiting for to change it?"

Good for: identifying where you're settling or avoiding

"If I imagine myself 5 years from now, having lived exactly as I'm living today — how does that future person feel? What do they wish I had started sooner?"

Good for: long-term perspective, urgency without panic

"What would I do differently if I knew I couldn't fail? What does that tell me about what I actually want?"

Good for: clarity on desires hidden behind fear

"What is the story I keep telling myself about why I can't have what I want? Is that story actually true?"

Good for: challenging limiting beliefs

"Who do I need to become to have the life I want? What's the gap between that person and who I am today?"

Good for: identity-level reflection, not just goal-setting

You don't need all of these every day. Pick two or three that resonate and make them yours. The best journaling practice is the one you keep coming back to — because it feels honest, not because it looks impressive.
