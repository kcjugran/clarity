How to Find Your Real Values

Life purpose isn't handed to you from outside — it's built from your values. And your real values aren't what you say matters. They're what you actually do.

By Karan Jugran, Living Foundations · Part of the Clarity series

Ask most people what they value and they'll give you a polished answer: family, health, growth. But watch how they spend their time and money and a different picture often emerges. Values live in behaviour, not in words. If you say you value health but never move your body, health isn't high on your value scale yet — no matter what you tell yourself.

What a value really is

A value is something you do automatically — no motivation required. That's the test. You don't have to talk yourself into it; you just do it. So to find your values, you don't introspect about ideals. You look at the evidence of your own life.

Watch your actual behaviour, not your stated beliefs. Where your time, money, and energy go without anyone pushing you — that's your true value system revealing itself.

The 5 clarity questions

These five questions take about 25 minutes total. Do them every day for at least two weeks, and don't reread yesterday's answers — start fresh each time. After two weeks, the answers that keep repeating are your true values.

  1. The money question. If you had a large sum to spend in one year (savings set aside), how would you divide it? Keep allocating until it's all spent.
  2. Unlimited wishes. If a genie granted unlimited wishes, what would you wish for? No filter, no judgment.
  3. Risky rewards. What do you deeply want but haven't done because fear is in the way? Name both the reward and the fear.
  4. Biggest pains. What's eating at you most right now — in relationships, health, money, work? Then rank them.
  5. The 6-month plan. Take your top three from each question above. With only six months to improve each, what would you do? End with one thing you can do today.

The most important line you'll write is the daily action at the end of question five. Keep it small and concrete — one call, one search, one email. Values become real the moment they turn into action.

Watch the evidence, not the excuses

Once you start looking at behaviour instead of words, the evidence is everywhere — including in your own house. Someone who genuinely values education tends to have a home full of books. Someone who values fitness has sports gear lying around. Someone who values aesthetics keeps their space looking good without being told to. None of this is judgment — it's just observation. The stuff you surround yourself with, without anyone reminding you to, is a value showing itself.

The same test works on people, not just possessions. Take two men who'd both tell you, in almost identical words, that they're devoted to their family. One leaves work early most evenings, sits with his family every night, and clears his weekends for them — when work and family collide, family wins. The other says the same things about loving his family, but takes no real time off, works from home even when he's physically there, and lets business win every time work and family collide. Listening to either man talk, you'd think they had the same priorities. Watching what they actually do tells you something different: the first man's real value is family; the second man's real value is his business, whatever he says out loud.

There's a simple reason this matters more than intentions: you're happiest and most capable in the areas that match your real values — the same way a fish thrives in water and a monkey thrives in trees, not the other way round. Living inside your actual values, not your stated ones, is what makes work and effort feel less like a grind and more like something you'd do anyway.

Turning your twelve points into six months

Once you've ranked your top three answers from each of the first four questions — money, wishes, risky rewards, and pain — you're holding twelve concrete points. The fifth question asks something specific of each one: if you had only six months to resolve it, or at least build a real plan for it, what would you actually do?

The shift this question forces is a shift in urgency. Most people handling a health problem, for instance, watch a couple of YouTube videos and ask a relative for advice — and then let it drag on for years. Give yourself a six-month deadline instead, and the answer changes: you'd find the best expert you could afford and work with them directly, and if that expert's approach didn't work, you'd find another one. That's a different level of seriousness than casual research.

If you had only six months to fix it, you wouldn't watch two YouTube videos and ask a relative — you'd hire the best expert you could afford and get to work.

Some of your twelve points genuinely can't be finished in six months — a full career change, for example. For those, the goal isn't completion, it's momentum: enroll in the course, have the real conversation, start saving toward it. Six months is enough to take the first serious step in every direction, even the ones that take years.

Key takeaways

  • Values show up as evidence, not claims. What fills your time, money, and home tells the truth before your words do.
  • Higher values need less motivation. The things you never have to be pushed into doing are sitting at the top of your real value system.
  • Answer fresh, not from memory. Never reread yesterday's entries before writing today's — repetition should emerge on its own, not be copied forward.
  • Two weeks is the minimum, not the goal. The first several days will wobble; consistency across the second week is what reveals the pattern.
  • Rank before you plan. Take the top three from each of the first four questions — twelve points total — before tackling the six-month question.
  • Urgency changes the plan. Ask what you'd do with only six months on a problem, and the answer gets a lot more decisive than "keep researching."

Frequently asked questions

What is a personal value, really?

A value is something you do automatically, without needing motivation. It shows up in your actual behaviour — how you spend time, money, and energy — not in what you say you believe.

How do you find your true values?

Watch what you do, not what you say. Answering five clarity questions every day for two weeks — without rereading yesterday's answers — reveals the repeating patterns that are your real values.

Why answer the questions daily instead of once?

Answering once gives you a snapshot shaped by today's mood. Answering daily for two weeks surfaces the patterns that persist — those are the values that actually drive you.

What if one of my twelve points can't be fixed in six months?

Aim for momentum instead of completion. For something like a full career change, six months is enough to enroll in a course, have the first real conversation, or start saving — the first serious step, not the finished result.
